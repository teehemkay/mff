The European Parliament’s role

The European Parliament plays a crucial role in safeguarding the EU's financial interests. This starts with approving the EU budget, both the long-term budget and the annual budgets.

  • This is done through the discharge procedure, which refers to the European Parliament's final approval of how the EU budget for a specific year has been implemented.

    It's an essential element of budgetary control and democratic oversight within the European Union. After an audition by the European Court of Auditors, Parliament's budgetary control committee examines the management of funds in the budget. The Parliament, upon recommendation by the committee, decides whether to grant, postpone, or refuse discharge (that is to say, give approval or not) to the European Commission or any other EU body for a given financial year. In essence, the discharge procedure ensures accountability and transparency by requiring those responsible for the implementation of the budget to justify their actions to the directly-elected European Parliament.

  • The Committe chairs are: Johan Van Overtveldt, Chair of the Budget Committee, and Niclas Herbst, Chair of the Budgetary Control Committee. The multiannual financial framework (MFF) raporteurs are Siegfried Mureșan and Carla Tavares. The own resources rapporteurs are Sandra Gomez Lopez and Danuše Nerudová.

    The rapporteur's key task is to analyse the project, consult with specialists in the particular field and with those who could be affected, discuss with other members within the committee and recommend ways forward.

  • This is done through the Rule of Law Conditionality Mechanism, which is an EU tool designed to protect the budget from misuse by member states. This preventive measure allows the European Commission to propose financial sanctions, such as suspending payments or reducing funding, if a member state's actions go against core EU values (for example, by increasing the risk of corruption or damaging judicial independence). The Council of the EU must approve these measures by a qualified majority.

    Affected member states can take corrective action to address the identified issues and potentially have the sanctions lifted. Ultimately, this mechanism strengthens the EU's ability to uphold the rule of law.

  • The European Parliament plays a crucial role in combatting fraud and safeguarding the EU's financial interests. Together with member states, it decides on the funding for the EU anti-fraud programme and adopts laws such as the directive on the protection of the EU’s financial interests.

    In this way, Parliament builds a strong legal framework against financial misconduct. The Parliament scrutinises the EU's anti-fraud actions by examining annual reports, holding hearings for key positions in oversight bodies such as the Court of Auditors and OLAF, and granting final approval for appointing leadership in these institutions. This active involvement underscores Parliament's commitment to transparency and accountability in managing EU funds.
